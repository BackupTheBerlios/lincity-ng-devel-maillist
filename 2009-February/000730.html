I'm in support of this too, adding that massively underfunded buildings
should incur some type of deterioration that will require repairs if they
actually want to get it working decently again -- thus, if they want to pull
a Bush and later on decided it was a bad idea, there should be additional
costs to repair the damaged infrastructure before being able to get it
working at the desired capacity again.

To clarify with an example implementation, if they reduce social spending
below some threshold, of say 40%, then there is a random chance each month
that deterioration begins (0% chance at 40%, 50% chance at 20%, 100% chance
at 0%, or "float chance = social_spending < 0.40f ? 0.40f - social_spending :
0.f" -- or it can be a logarithm).  So once deterioration begins, the
infrastructure has a random chance of deteriorating each month to a minimum
capacity of the amount they reduced social spending to.  Again, the chance
that it will deteriorate each month should be based upon how underfunded they
are.  Thus, to increase it beyond point that deterioration has reached, they
would have to pay the adjusted cost of the buildings again, or destroy and
completely rebuild them (in case they want to move them anyway).
